## Everyone Is "Using AI." How Many Are Using It Well?

There is a familiar gap between people who know git and people who use git well. The first group does `git add . && git commit -m "fix"` and pushes. The second knows when to rebase and when to merge, keeps the commit graph in their head, and treats commit messages as documentation. Both will tell you they "know git." The operation is very different.

Something similar is happening with AI. Most people open a chat window, ask something, get something back, and paste it somewhere. Fewer know when to bring AI into a workflow, how to communicate with it clearly, how to evaluate what comes back, and how much accountability they carry for what comes out the other end.

That gap is what the **4D Framework** is designed to address. Developed through a research collaboration between Professor Rick Dakan at Ringling College of Art and Design and Professor Joseph Feller at University College Cork, it describes AI fluency as four interconnected competencies.

## The Four Ds

The framework identifies: **Delegation**, **Description**, **Discernment**, and **Diligence**. Four abstract nouns in a row tends to induce a certain glaze. Stay with it — the content earns the alliteration.

## Delegation: Deciding Who Does What

The first D is about task distribution. What should go to AI, what should stay with a human, and how do you think strategically about that choice?

Think of it this way: asking a colleague to *refactor this function* is fundamentally different from asking them to *fix the typo in the commit message and push*. One requires independent judgment, the other is mechanical execution. AI draws the same distinction — and it matters which side of the line you put your task on.

Delegation is less "what can AI do?" and more "is AI the right tool for *this* task?" Those are different questions, and the second one requires an honest answer. Handing a language model a task that needs reliable factual grounding, careful human judgment, or domain expertise you cannot verify — that is a delegation failure. The tool did not misrepresent its limitations. You misassigned the work.

## Description: What You Say Shapes What You Get

The second D covers communication — how you define outputs, guide the process, and specify the behavior you need.

You have probably heard "prompt engineering" mentioned. It has followed a trajectory similar to "cloud engineer" circa 2015: a real skill that rapidly became a title anyone could claim after opening a console and clicking a few buttons. The concept is legitimate; the bar for claiming fluency dropped very fast.

Real Description skill is not about memorizing prompt templates. It is about thinking clearly enough about what you want to actually specify it. GIGO — Garbage In, Garbage Out — has been a true principle in computing since 1957. Language models did not repeal it. They just made the garbage come out more fluently, more confidently, and occasionally in beautifully formatted markdown.

Good Description means defining outputs as concrete goals, not vague topics. It means stating constraints explicitly. It means asking "what output do I need?" before asking "what question should I type?"

## Discernment: Think Before You Paste

The third D is critical evaluation of AI outputs — quality, accuracy, appropriateness. Does what you got actually deliver on what you needed?

LLMs hallucinate. This is worth saying plainly: models generate confident-sounding text that can be completely fabricated. Sources that do not exist. Papers that were never written. Library methods that have never shipped in any version. The model does this with the same calm authority it uses when it is completely right.

Discernment is what catches this. Goodhart's Law applies here: *"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."* Accepting AI output because it looks complete, sounds fluent, and has well-formatted bullet points is exactly that failure mode. You optimized for surface coherence and forgot to check for truth. The output passed the vibe check and failed the fact check.

Good Discernment asks: Is this accurate? Is it appropriate for the context? Where is it falling short? What would make it better? Not skepticism for its own sake — the editorial process that separates using AI from using AI well.

## Diligence: Who Is Accountable?

The fourth D gets the least attention and probably matters the most: responsible and ethical use. Transparency, accountability, and making thoughtful choices about which AI systems you use and when.

"The AI did it" is not a defense. This is the same principle as "the compiler generated it" — technically accurate and professionally meaningless. If you commit the code, you own the code. If you submit the analysis, you own the analysis. AI is a tool. Accountability stays with the person holding it.

Diligence also covers transparency: being clear about when you used AI, what role it played, and where it shaped the work. In team contexts, this is basic professional communication. In high-stakes contexts, it is the baseline for trust.

It also means making deliberate choices about which systems to use. Models differ in capability, safety guarantees, data handling, and appropriate use cases. Treating all AI tools as interchangeable is like treating all databases as interchangeable — theoretically defensible, operationally a recipe for problems.

## The Four Together

What makes the 4D Framework useful is that the competencies are not independent. Strong Delegation means you hand off the right tasks. Strong Description means the handoff is clearly communicated. Strong Discernment means you evaluate what comes back honestly. Strong Diligence means the whole process stays accountable.

Developing all four changes the question from "do I use AI?" to "how well do I actually work with AI?" That shift is meaningful — practically and professionally.

## AI Fluency as a Baseline Skill

"AI literacy" has become a phrase stretched to cover everything from opening a chat window to building fine-tuned pipelines. At its thinnest, it means being able to type a question and get a response. The 4D Framework sets a higher bar: not just operating AI tools, but operating them thoughtfully.

The four competencies — Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence — are not exotic. They are the habits a good engineer, a good scientist, and a good collaborator already try to build. AI makes them more visible, more urgent, and more worth being deliberate about.

The framework came out of research at Ringling College and University College Cork, but the questions it raises are universal. And the earlier you start asking them, the sooner "I use AI" turns into something that actually means something.

The gap between those two things is not small.
