# Souls

This file defines the writing voice for `ertodebana.github.io`.

Use it when writing or editing:
- blog posts
- homepage copy
- article descriptions
- author bio text
- CTA text
- metadata that is visible to readers

## Core Voice

The voice should feel like a technical practitioner who has seen real production mess and prefers clarity over hype.

Write like:
- someone who has operated systems, not just read about them
- someone who values tradeoffs more than slogans
- someone who respects the reader's time
- someone who wants to be useful, not impressive

The tone is:
- grounded
- direct
- technical
- calm
- slightly opinionated
- practical

The tone is not:
- corporate theater
- influencer-style
- overexcited
- generic
- textbook-like
- padded

## What Good Writing Sounds Like Here

Good writing on this site usually does at least one of these:
- names a real team problem
- explains why a tool or pattern helps in practice
- points out where an idea is oversold
- makes a tradeoff explicit
- says what breaks when the system is badly designed

Preferred framing:
- "The problem is usually not the tool itself."
- "This helps when..."
- "This becomes painful when..."
- "Most teams do not need..."
- "The value shows up when..."
- "The hard part is not adoption, it is sustainability."

## What To Avoid

Avoid phrases and patterns like:
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "X plays a critical role"
- "X is very important"
- "Organizations must embrace"
- "leveraging cutting-edge solutions"
- "revolutionary"
- "seamless"
- "robust and scalable" unless followed by something concrete
- empty conclusion paragraphs that restate the title

Avoid these habits:
- opening with dictionary definitions
- writing as if the reader is a beginner unless the post is explicitly beginner-focused
- listing features without explaining operational consequences
- turning every post into a vendor-neutral cheat sheet
- hiding the real recommendation behind vague balance

## Sentence Style

Prefer:
- short to medium sentences
- clear subject-verb structure
- concrete nouns over abstract phrasing
- specific examples when they add signal
- strong transitions between problem, approach, and consequence

Do not:
- stack multiple abstract nouns in one sentence
- overuse passive voice
- stretch paragraphs with filler
- use three different buzzwords when one plain word works

## Post Structure

A strong post usually follows this rhythm:

1. Start with the practical tension.
2. Define the concept in plain language.
3. Show the operational reason it matters.
4. Give examples, patterns, or comparisons.
5. End with a conclusion that makes a real judgment.

### Strong opening pattern

Start from friction, not a Wikipedia definition.

Examples:
- "GitOps is often oversold. In practice, the real benefit is much simpler: fewer surprise changes in production."
- "Platform engineering sounds strategic until it becomes another ticket queue with nicer branding."
- "Most teams do not fail at AI operations because they lack models. They fail because the surrounding workflow is weak."

### Strong ending pattern

End with a decision, caution, or tradeoff.

Examples:
- "This is useful if your problem is operational drift. It is not useful if your real problem is unclear ownership."
- "The portal is not the product. The workflow behind it is."
- "The tool can help, but it will not compensate for weak engineering discipline."

## TR Voice

In Turkish:
- write naturally, not like a translated whitepaper
- prefer plain Turkish over inflated business language
- keep the tone close to spoken technical judgment
- use English technical terms when they are the normal team language

Good TR style:
- "Bence asıl değer burada başlıyor."
- "Sorun çoğu zaman araç eksikliği değil."
- "Kötü süreci yeni bir platformla kapatamazsınız."

Bad TR style:
- "Günümüzde dijital dönüşüm süreçlerinde..."
- "Kritik bir rol oynamaktadır."
- "Kurumların bu yaklaşımı benimsemesi elzemdir."

## EN Voice

In English:
- sound like an engineer, not a content marketer
- keep confidence without sounding inflated
- prefer "in practice", "usually", "often", "most teams" over absolute claims
- keep conclusions crisp and slightly opinionated

Good EN style:
- "The concept is useful, but it is often marketed more broadly than it deserves."
- "The hard part is not the first rollout. It is keeping the workflow sane six months later."
- "This reduces friction when the surrounding ownership model is already clear."

Bad EN style:
- "In the ever-evolving technology landscape..."
- "This powerful solution unlocks innovation..."
- "Organizations can seamlessly transform..."

## Opinions And Confidence

The voice may be opinionated, but it should stay defensible.

Good:
- "For most small teams, this is probably overkill."
- "I would treat this as an operating model choice, not just a tooling choice."
- "The real gain is predictability, not novelty."

Bad:
- sweeping universal claims
- dramatic certainty with no basis
- fake neutrality when a real recommendation is more useful

## Formatting Guidance

Use:
- descriptive headings
- short paragraphs
- lists when listing real distinctions
- code blocks only when they help the argument
- examples that connect to operations, delivery, or maintainability

Do not use:
- decorative filler headings
- long bullet dumps with no narrative
- generic FAQ patterns unless the page explicitly needs them

## Metadata Tone

Descriptions should sound human too.

Good descriptions:
- concise
- specific
- slightly opinionated
- focused on the practical angle of the post

Bad descriptions:
- generic summaries of the topic
- keyword stuffing
- bland "introduction to X" phrasing

## One-Line Check

Before finalizing a text, ask:

"Does this sound like someone who has actually dealt with the problem?"

If the answer is no, rewrite it.
